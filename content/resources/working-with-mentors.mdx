Mentors are one of the most valuable assets of the fellowship — and the most underused. This guide shows you how to get maximum value from your mentor relationship.

## What mentors have committed to

Mentors volunteer approximately **2–4 hours per month** across the fellowship. This typically looks like:
- One 30 or 60-minute video call per week (scheduled by the team)
- Occasional async questions via email or Slack
- One demo prep session in week 9

They are not available for daily support. They are available for strategic guidance, connections, and perspective you can't get anywhere else.

## What mentors are NOT

- **Not your project manager.** They won't chase you for updates or tell you what to do next.
- **Not your technical team.** They won't write code or build the product for you.
- **Not investors (necessarily).** Some mentors may become investors in your project, but that's not their role during the fellowship.
- **Not your employees.** They're doing this because they want to. Treat their time accordingly.

## How to prepare for mentor meetings

The teams that get the most out of mentorship come to every meeting with **one specific question or decision they need help making**.

Bad: "Can you give us an update on our project direction?"

Good: "We've narrowed down to two MVP scopes — [option A] and [option B]. Here's why each has merit and here's what we're unsure about. We'd love 20 minutes on which path you'd take and why."

Send your agenda 24 hours in advance. A two-sentence email is enough:
> "Looking forward to our call tomorrow. We'd like your input on [specific question] and [secondary question if there's time]. Here's a quick summary of where we are: [2–3 sentences]."

## Running the meeting

- **Start with updates quickly** — 5 minutes max. They don't need the full story; they need just enough context to advise.
- **Get to your questions.** This is where the value is.
- **Take notes.** Assign someone to take notes and share them with the mentor afterward. It shows professionalism and ensures you remember what was said.
- **End with clear next steps.** "We'll do X by [date] and report back" is a much stronger close than "Thanks, we'll think about it."

## Getting introductions

One of the highest-value things a mentor can do is introduce you to the right person. Don't be shy about asking.

How to ask:
> "We've been struggling to get access to [type of user] for our research. Do you know anyone who works in [context] who might be willing to talk to us for 20 minutes? We're specifically trying to understand [specific question]."

Be specific. "Do you know anyone in healthcare?" is too vague. "Do you know any hospitalists who've been involved in a health IT implementation?" is specific enough to act on.

## When things aren't working with your mentor

Most mentor relationships work well, but occasionally there's a mismatch. Signs of a poor fit:
- Your mentor doesn't have relevant domain knowledge for your problem
- Response time is consistently very slow
- Your project has pivoted away from the area the mentor was matched for

If this happens: don't ghost. Send a brief, honest note:
> "We've really appreciated your time. Our project has evolved toward [new direction] and we think we might benefit most from connecting with someone who has [specific experience]. Would it be okay if we transitioned to a different mentor relationship?"

Then contact your program facilitator to discuss re-matching. It happens and it's not a problem.

## Types of mentors and how to use them

**Clinical domain mentors** (physician, nurse, allied health)
Best for: Validating that the problem is real, understanding clinical workflow, identifying the right user persona, reviewing clinical accuracy of your outputs.
Ask them: "Does this reflect how real clinicians work?" "What would make a clinician actually adopt this?" "Who else should I talk to?"

**Technical mentors** (engineers, CTOs, technical founders)
Best for: Technology architecture decisions, debugging, evaluating technical feasibility, identifying when a simpler approach exists.
Ask them: "Is our technical approach reasonable?" "What would you do differently?" "Are we over-engineering this?"

**Product mentors** (PMs, designers, founders)
Best for: MVP scoping, user research design, product strategy, prioritization.
Ask them: "Is our MVP scope too big?" "What's the core user story we're optimizing for?" "How would you approach validating this?"

**Industry/business mentors** (investors, operators, founders)
Best for: Business model, go-to-market strategy, regulatory navigation, fundraising, Demo Day prep.
Ask them: "Who would pay for this and why?" "What does the regulatory path look like?" "How would you pitch this to a health system CIO?"

## A note on reciprocity

Mentors are investing time because they care about the next generation of healthcare innovators. The best way to honor that is to:

- Be on time for meetings
- Follow through on what you say you'll do
- Share outcomes — tell them how your project progressed, what happened at Demo Day
- Express gratitude explicitly and specifically (not just "thanks" — "your introduction to [person] led to [specific insight] that changed our product direction")

Many of the best professional relationships in healthcare come from fellowship programs. Treat this as the beginning of a long relationship, not a transactional exchange.
