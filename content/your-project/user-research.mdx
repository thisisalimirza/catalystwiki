User research means talking to real people who have the problem you're trying to solve, before you build anything. It's the most valuable thing you can do in weeks 1–2 and it will dramatically change what you decide to build.

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't skip this">
The most common feedback we give to Catalyst teams in week 6 is "you should have talked to more users before building." Save yourself the pain — do the research first.
</Callout>

## Who to talk to

For most healthcare problems, your user pool is one of:

- **Clinicians** — Physicians, NPs, PAs, nurses, pharmacists, therapists
- **Patients** — People with a specific condition or care experience
- **Healthcare administrators/operators** — People who manage clinical workflows, scheduling, billing
- **Caregivers** — Family members managing care for someone else
- **Researchers** — If your problem is research-facing

Be specific. "Clinicians" is too broad. "Emergency medicine attendings at urban academic medical centers" is specific enough.

## How to find people to interview

**You already know people.** If you're a medical student, resident, or clinician, you have colleagues, attendings, and classmates. Start there. 5 informal conversations with people you already know are better than 0 formal interviews with strangers.

**Use your sponsor.** If you're on the sponsored track, ask your sponsor contact for introductions to 2–3 people inside the organization who experience the problem.

**Use your mentor.** Mentors often have networks that include exactly the people you need to talk to. Ask for introductions.

**Recruit via your institution.** If you need patient perspectives and you're at a hospital or academic medical center, your IRB office can advise on whether your interviews need ethical review. For conceptual user research (not involving medical records or identifiable health information), you often don't need IRB approval — but check.

<Callout type="info" title="IRB for user research">
For Catalyst purposes, user research interviews that don't involve accessing patient records, don't involve vulnerable populations, and are purely conversational typically don't require IRB approval. When in doubt, ask your institution's IRB or your mentor.
</Callout>

## How to run an interview

**Keep it to 30–45 minutes.** Respect people's time.

**Don't pitch your idea.** This is the biggest mistake. The goal is to understand their experience, not validate your solution. If you pitch your idea, people will evaluate your solution instead of telling you about their problem.

**Ask about the past, not the hypothetical.** "Tell me about the last time you had to handle a complex discharge" is 10x more valuable than "Would you use a tool that...?"

**Follow the interesting threads.** Your interview guide is a starting point. If someone says something unexpected, follow it. "Can you say more about that?" is your most useful phrase.

### Starter interview questions (adapt to your domain)

**Opening (understand their context)**
- "Can you tell me about your current role and what a typical day looks like?"
- "How long have you been doing this?"

**Core problem exploration**
- "Tell me about the last time [the problem you're investigating] came up."
- "Walk me through exactly what you did."
- "What was the hardest part?"
- "What do you usually do when that happens?"
- "Have you tried any tools or workarounds? What happened?"

**Impact and stakes**
- "How often does this happen?"
- "What's the consequence when it goes wrong?"
- "How much time does this add to your day?"

**Closing**
- "If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about [this situation], what would it be?"
- "Is there anyone else I should talk to about this?"

## How to take notes

Assign one person to take notes — not the interviewer. The interviewer should maintain eye contact and focus on following threads.

After the interview, immediately write a 2–3 paragraph summary of:
1. What surprised you most
2. The biggest pain point they mentioned
3. Any quotes that stood out

Do this within 30 minutes of the interview — you'll forget key details fast.

## How to synthesize what you learned

After 3–5 interviews, look for patterns:

1. Write each distinct pain point on a sticky note (real or digital — try FigJam or Miro)
2. Group related pain points together
3. Look for the pattern that appears across the most interviews
4. Ask: "Which of these is the most painful? Which is most frequent?"

The problem worth solving is usually the one that is **both painful AND frequent**. A problem that's catastrophic but happens once a year is harder to build for than one that's moderately annoying and happens every single shift.

## Documenting your research

Submit a brief user research summary (3–5 bullet points per interview + your synthesis) as your Week 3 milestone. Format doesn't matter — clarity does.

Template:
```
Interview 1: [Role], [Years in role], [Setting]
- Key pain points: ...
- Biggest surprise: ...
- Notable quote: "..."

[Repeat for each interview]

Synthesis:
- Common pattern: ...
- Problem statement (updated): ...
- What we're going to build (initial hypothesis): ...
```
