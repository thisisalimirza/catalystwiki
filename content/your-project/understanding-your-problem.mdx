The most common reason Catalyst projects fall short isn't technical — it's that teams started building before they truly understood the problem. This guide will help you avoid that.

## The fundamental mistake

Most people — especially clinicians — think they already understand the problem because they see it every day. But there's a crucial difference between:

- "I see patients struggle to understand their discharge instructions" (observation)
- "Patients who don't understand discharge instructions are 2x more likely to be readmitted, and the bottleneck is that instructions are written at a 12th-grade reading level while our average patient reads at a 6th-grade level" (understood problem)

The second version tells you exactly what to build. The first version might lead you to build something that solves the wrong part of the problem.

## The problem framing framework

Use this structure to articulate your problem before you start building:

### 1. Who has this problem?
Be specific. "Patients" is too broad. "ICU nurses during shift changes at a 500-bed academic medical center" is specific enough to design for.

- Who is the primary person experiencing the pain?
- What are they doing when the problem occurs?
- What is their context (time pressure, environment, expertise level)?

### 2. What is the current situation?
Describe what happens today, without your solution in the picture.

- What does the person do right now when this problem comes up?
- What workarounds have they invented?
- What tools do they already use?

### 3. What is the cost of the problem?
Problems worth solving have real, measurable costs. Identify at least one:

- **Clinical cost:** Patient harm, misdiagnosis, delayed treatment
- **Time cost:** How many minutes/hours per day is this consuming?
- **Financial cost:** Avoidable readmissions, unnecessary tests, wasted staff time
- **Emotional cost:** Burnout, moral injury, patient distress

### 4. What would "solved" look like?
Describe what success looks like for the user — before you describe your solution.

"The nurse would be able to complete a shift handoff that captures all critical patient information in under 3 minutes, without having to dig through multiple systems."

### 5. What are the constraints?
What makes this hard to solve? These are not obstacles to ignore — they're the shape of the problem.

- Regulatory (HIPAA, FDA, state licensing)
- Workflow (can't add 10 minutes to an already slammed schedule)
- Technical (legacy EHR systems with no API access)
- Social (physicians won't use a tool they didn't choose)

<Callout type="tip" title="Write it down">
Completing this framework takes about 30–60 minutes of focused thinking. Write it in a shared doc with your team. Come back to it after your user research and update it. It will change — that's good.
</Callout>

## For sponsored track fellows

Your sponsor has given you a problem brief. Read it carefully, but don't treat it as the final word. The brief represents what the sponsor *thinks* the problem is — which may or may not be what the actual users experience.

Your job in weeks 1–2 is to **validate the problem brief against what you hear from real users**. If the brief says "clinicians struggle with X" and you talk to 5 clinicians who never mention X, you need to go back to your sponsor contact with that finding — and that's valuable.

## For indie track fellows

You're starting with an idea. Now you need to stress-test it. Ask yourself:

- "Have I personally experienced this problem as a clinician/patient/researcher?" — If yes, great starting point. But you're not your user.
- "Who else has this problem?" — Can I name 10 real people who have it right now?
- "Why hasn't it been solved?" — What's made this hard? (There's usually a real reason.)
- "Is there a regulation or incumbent that blocks solutions?" — Know what you're navigating.

## User research: your actual tool

The only reliable way to understand a problem is to talk to people who have it. Before you start building, talk to at least **3–5 people** who are the target users of your solution.

See the [User research guide](/your-project/user-research) for exactly how to do this — including interview scripts, what questions to ask, and how to synthesize what you learn.

## One final check

Before you move to scoping your MVP, answer these three questions:

1. "I am building for [specific person] who needs to [specific job], because [current situation causes this specific pain]."
2. "I've spoken to at least 3 people who confirmed they have this problem and that the pain I identified is real."
3. "If I solve this one specific thing, the cost of the problem goes down in this specific, measurable way."

If you can answer all three clearly, you're ready to define what you'll build.
