## What is Sales, Really?

If you've never sold anything before, the word *sales* might conjure up images of pushy car dealerships or cold calls nobody wants to receive. Set that image aside.

At its core, sales is the process of connecting a solution to someone who has a real problem — and helping them understand why your solution is worth their time, trust, and money. For a first-time founder, especially one coming from medicine, this reframe matters. You're not manipulating anyone. You're having conversations, listening carefully, and figuring out whether what you've built actually fits what someone needs.

<Callout type="info" title="A Note for Catalyst Fellows">
The goal of the Catalyst Fellowship is **validation and learning** — not necessarily securing a paying customer. You are here to understand a problem deeply, build an MVP, and test your assumptions. This page exists for teams who want to go above and beyond and hit the ground running after the fellowship. Don't let the complexity here intimidate you. Treat it as a resource you can grow into.
</Callout>

## Core Sales Vocabulary

Before diving into strategy, it's worth building a shared language. These terms will appear throughout this page and in almost every conversation you'll have with investors, mentors, and customers.

| Term | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Lead** | Anyone who might potentially be interested in your product. Unqualified — you don't yet know if they're a real fit. |
| **Prospect** | A lead you've vetted and believe has a genuine need for your product. |
| **Pipeline** | Your full collection of active prospects at various stages of the sales process. |
| **Conversion** | When a prospect takes a desired action — signing up, paying, agreeing to a pilot. |
| **Churn** | When a customer stops using or paying for your product. High churn means something isn't working. |
| **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)** | How much you spend on average to acquire one new customer. Includes marketing, time, and sales effort. |
| **LTV (Lifetime Value)** | How much revenue a single customer generates over their entire relationship with you. You want LTV to be much higher than CAC. |
| **Economic Buyer** | The person who controls the budget and makes the final purchase decision. Often not the person who uses the product. |
| **Champion** | Someone inside an organization who believes in your product and advocates for it internally on your behalf. |
| **Pilot** | A time-limited, often lower-commitment trial of your product within an organization — common in B2B healthcare sales. |
| **Procurement** | The formal process an organization uses to evaluate, approve, and purchase new vendors or software. |
| **Discovery Call** | An early conversation with a prospect focused on understanding their problems, not pitching your product. |
| **Objection** | A concern or hesitation a prospect raises. Not a rejection — an opportunity to clarify and address concerns. |
| **Closing** | The moment you ask for and receive a commitment — a signature, a payment, a formal agreement to proceed. |

<Callout type="tip" title="You Already Know How to Do Discovery">
As a medical student, you've been trained to take histories — to ask open-ended questions, listen carefully, and synthesize what a patient is telling you. A discovery call works the same way. You're diagnosing a problem before prescribing a solution.
</Callout>

## Two Paths: B2B vs. B2C

Most healthcare tech startups fall into one of two sales models, and it's worth understanding which one you're in — because they require very different approaches.

**B2B (Business-to-Business)** means you're selling to an organization — a hospital, a clinic, a payer (insurance company), an employer, or a health system. The customer is an institution, not an individual. Sales cycles are long, decision-making involves multiple people, and deals are typically high-value.

**B2C (Business-to-Consumer)** means you're selling directly to individuals — patients, caregivers, or consumers managing their own health. Deals are lower value per person, but you can reach many more people, and decisions happen much faster.

Some products have both motions — for example, a mental health app might sell directly to consumers and sell enterprise licenses to employers or health systems. This is sometimes called a **B2B2C** model.

<Callout type="warning" title="Know Which Model You're In">
Trying to run a B2B and B2C sales motion simultaneously as a first-time founder with limited time is extremely difficult. If your product could serve both, start by picking one. Validate it. Then expand.
</Callout>

## B2C Sales in Healthcare

### The Framework

Selling directly to consumers in healthcare is, in some ways, more familiar — you're talking to individuals, just like interacting with patients or peers. But healthcare consumers are a unique audience. They are often vulnerable, skeptical of new technology, and cautious about who they trust with their health data.

The B2C sales framework for healthcare comes down to four stages:

1. **Awareness** — Does your target customer know you exist?
2. **Trust** — Do they believe you are credible and safe?
3. **Activation** — Do they try your product?
4. **Retention** — Do they keep using it, and do they tell others?

Each stage has different levers. Most first-time founders over-invest in awareness and under-invest in trust and retention.

### Your Target Customer

Before doing anything else, get specific about who your customer is. Not "patients" — that's too broad. Instead: "Adults over 60 managing type 2 diabetes who are comfortable with smartphones but not tech-savvy." The more specific your customer profile, the easier every other decision becomes.

Ask yourself:

- Where do they spend time online and offline?
- What do they already trust? (Their doctor? A specific publication? A community group?)
- What would make them hesitant to try something new?
- What does their day look like? When would they actually use your product?

### Building Trust First

In healthcare, trust is your most important asset. Consumers need to believe that your product is safe, private, and backed by credible expertise. A few ways to build it early:

- **Clinician credibility** — Being a medical student is actually an asset here. Lead with it. Your clinical background signals legitimacy.
- **Transparent privacy practices** — Be explicit about what data you collect and what you do with it. Don't bury it in a terms of service page.
- **Social proof** — Early testimonials, even from beta users, carry weight. A quote from a real person is more powerful than any feature description.
- **Partnership signals** — Logos of affiliated hospitals, universities, or programs (like Catalyst) tell consumers you're not operating alone in the dark.

### Tactical B2C Playbook

**Finding your first users:**

- Start with your immediate network — classmates, family, patients you've encountered in clinical settings (with appropriate care around solicitation)
- Post in condition-specific online communities (subreddits, Facebook groups, patient advocacy forums) — lead with genuine participation, not promotion
- Partner with patient advocacy organizations who already have your audience's trust
- Consider using waitlist pages to build early interest before your product is ready

**Conversion tactics:**

- Reduce friction to zero for the first action — free sign-up, no credit card, instant value
- Use a clear and singular call to action — don't give people five things to click on
- Address the most common objection upfront: "Is my data safe?" Answer it before they ask

**Retention:**

- The single best retention tool is a product that genuinely solves a problem — no marketing trick compensates for a weak product
- Build in moments that remind users of the value they've gotten (progress summaries, check-ins, milestones)
- Ask for feedback early and often — users who feel heard are far more likely to stay

**Metrics to watch:**

- **Activation rate** — What % of sign-ups actually use the product in the first week?
- **Day 7 / Day 30 retention** — Are people still around after a week? A month?
- **Net Promoter Score (NPS)** — Would your users recommend you? This is a leading indicator of word-of-mouth growth.

## B2B Sales in Healthcare

This is where things get significantly more complex — and where most first-time founders underestimate what they're getting into. Healthcare organizations are among the most difficult institutions to sell to in any industry. Procurement is slow, stakeholders are many, and the cost of getting it wrong (clinically, legally, financially) makes organizations cautious by default.

That said, B2B healthcare deals are also high-value and sticky. A single hospital system contract can be worth more than thousands of consumer subscriptions. Understanding how this world works is a genuine competitive advantage.

### The Framework: Who Actually Makes the Decision?

The single most important insight in B2B healthcare sales is this: **the person who uses your product is almost never the person who buys it.**

A nurse might love your clinical workflow tool. But the nurse doesn't control the budget. The nurse's manager might be enthusiastic. But they need sign-off from the CMO. The CMO needs IT security review. IT needs legal to look at the BAA. Legal needs procurement to run the vendor assessment. And somewhere in there, the CFO wants to know the ROI.

This is called a **buying committee**, and navigating it is the central challenge of B2B healthcare sales.

The key stakeholders you'll typically encounter:

| Stakeholder | Their Primary Concern |
|---|---|
| **Clinical Champion** | Does this solve a real clinical problem? Is it easy to use? |
| **Department Head / Medical Director** | Will this make my team better? Is the evidence solid? |
| **CMO / CNO** | Does this align with our strategic priorities? |
| **CIO / IT Department** | Is this secure? Does it integrate with our EHR? |
| **Legal / Compliance** | Are we HIPAA compliant? What's our liability? |
| **Procurement** | Is this vendor legitimate? What are the contract terms? |
| **CFO / Finance** | What does this cost? What's the return? |

Your job is to understand who is in the room, what each person cares about, and how to give each of them a reason to say yes — or at least not say no.

### The B2B Healthcare Sales Cycle

Unlike B2C, where a customer can decide in minutes, B2B healthcare sales cycles commonly take **6 to 18 months** from first conversation to signed contract. Here is a typical progression:

1. **Identify and research your target accounts** — Which organizations have the problem you solve? Who inside them is most likely to care?
2. **Get a warm introduction** — Cold outreach works poorly in healthcare. Find a mutual connection, attend the same conference, or come through an accelerator or academic partner.
3. **Run a discovery call** — Ask questions, don't pitch. Understand their workflows, their pain points, their current solutions, and what's already been tried.
4. **Identify your champion** — Who inside the organization is excited about what you're doing? This person will sell for you internally when you're not in the room.
5. **Map the buying committee** — Ask your champion directly: "Who else would need to be involved in a decision like this?"
6. **Present a proposal** — Tailor it to the organization. Address each stakeholder's concerns. Include a clear pilot structure.
7. **Run a pilot** — A time-limited, defined test of your product with clear success criteria agreed upon upfront.
8. **Navigate procurement and legal** — Budget approvals, vendor assessments, HIPAA Business Associate Agreements (BAAs), contract negotiations.
9. **Close and onboard** — Sign the contract, implement the product, and ensure the team actually adopts it.
10. **Expand** — A successful pilot in one department becomes the blueprint for expansion to others.

<Callout type="warning" title="The Pilot Trap">
Many first-time founders celebrate landing a pilot as if it were a sale. It is not. Pilots stall, get deprioritized, and quietly die all the time — especially in large health systems where your champion gets reassigned or leadership priorities shift. Always negotiate pilots with **defined success criteria, a clear timeline, and an explicit next step** if those criteria are met. A pilot without a path to purchase is a distraction.
</Callout>

### Your Champion is Everything

In B2B healthcare, your champion — the internal advocate who believes in your product — is your most valuable asset. Cultivate this relationship carefully.

A good champion:

- Has credibility within the organization
- Experiences the problem you solve firsthand
- Is willing to put their name behind recommending you
- Will help you understand internal politics and timing

A few things to keep in mind:

- Champions get reassigned, promoted, or leave. Never make your entire deal dependent on one person.
- Help your champion make the internal case. Give them slides, data, and talking points they can use when you're not in the room.
- Check in regularly, but respect their time. They are doing this in addition to their day job.

### HIPAA and Compliance as a Sales Reality

If your product touches patient data in any way, you will need to address HIPAA compliance as part of every B2B conversation. This is non-negotiable and not something to defer.

Key concepts to understand:

- **PHI (Protected Health Information)** — Any data that could identify a patient. If your product handles PHI, you have significant compliance obligations.
- **BAA (Business Associate Agreement)** — A legally required contract between your company and any covered entity (hospital, clinic, payer) that shares PHI with you. You will need to sign one before any serious B2B deal can close.
- **SOC 2 Compliance** — A security audit framework that enterprise customers increasingly require before they'll work with a software vendor.

<Callout type="info" title="Compliance is a Feature, Not Just a Checkbox">
First-time founders often see HIPAA as a hurdle. Savvy founders use it as a selling point. Being able to say *"We are HIPAA-compliant, we have our BAA template ready, and we've passed IT security reviews at other institutions"* removes one of the biggest objections large organizations have. Invest in getting this right early.
</Callout>

### Pricing Your B2B Product

Pricing in B2B healthcare is less about a number on a website and more about a conversation. Common models include:

- **Per seat / per user** — A monthly or annual fee per clinician or staff member using the product
- **Per patient / per member** — Common for population health tools; priced based on the number of patients managed
- **Site license** — A flat fee for an entire department or facility, regardless of number of users
- **Outcomes-based / risk-share** — You get paid based on measurable results (e.g., reduced readmissions). Sophisticated, hard to negotiate early on, but compelling to risk-bearing organizations.

<Callout type="tip" title="Don't Be Afraid to Charge">
Medical student founders often underprice dramatically out of fear of rejection. But in B2B healthcare, a price that seems too low can actually undermine trust. If a hospital is paying $500 for something that touches clinical workflows, they're going to wonder what corners you've cut. Price signals quality and sustainability.
</Callout>

### Tactical B2B Playbook

**Getting your first conversations:**

- Leverage your medical school network — attendings, residents, and department chiefs are your first doors into health systems
- Use the Catalyst Fellowship's institutional relationships and mentors to get warm introductions
- Attend healthcare conferences (HIMSS, HLTH, specialty society meetings) where decision-makers gather
- Join relevant LinkedIn communities and engage genuinely before reaching out

**Running a great discovery call:**

- Prepare 5–7 open-ended questions before the call
- Spend at least 70% of the call listening, not talking
- Ask: "What have you already tried? Why didn't it work?"
- End every call with a clear next step — never let it end with "let's stay in touch"

**Moving deals forward:**

- Send a follow-up email within 24 hours summarizing what was discussed and confirming the next step
- If a deal goes quiet, follow up. Then follow up again. Persistence (not pestering) is part of the job.
- When in doubt, ask your champion: "What would need to be true for this to move forward?"

**Structuring a pilot:**

- Define success criteria upfront and get the stakeholder to agree to them in writing
- Keep pilots short and focused — 60 to 90 days is usually enough to demonstrate value
- Assign a point of contact on both sides responsible for the pilot's success
- Schedule a formal readout meeting at the end before the pilot begins

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfalls for Everyone

- **Pitching before listening** — Founders who lead with their product before understanding the customer's problem almost always miss the mark. Ask first.
- **Confusing interest with intent** — Someone saying "this is really cool" is not the same as someone saying "I will pay for this." Validate intent explicitly.
- **Avoiding the money conversation** — Many first-time founders are uncomfortable talking about price. Get comfortable with it early.
