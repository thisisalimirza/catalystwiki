Prompt engineering is the skill of writing instructions to AI models that produce reliable, high-quality outputs. It sounds technical but it's really just learning how to communicate well with a very powerful tool. This is the highest-leverage skill you can learn for the fellowship.

## The mental model

Think of an AI model as an extremely capable new employee who:
- Has read virtually everything on the internet
- Has no memory of previous conversations (unless you give them context)
- Will try to do exactly what you ask — so be precise
- Will fill in gaps with assumptions — so be explicit
- Performs much better with examples than with rules

Good prompting is just good communication.

## The anatomy of a prompt

Every interaction with an AI model has up to three components:

**1. System prompt** (optional but important)
The standing instructions. This sets the persona, rules, format, and constraints that apply to every message. Think of it as the job description.

**2. User message**
The specific task or question in this conversation.

**3. Examples** (optional but powerful)
Sample inputs and outputs that show the model what "good" looks like. This is called few-shot prompting.

## Core techniques

### Be explicit about format
If you want a table, say "return a table." If you want bullet points, say "use bullet points." If you want JSON, say "return JSON."

**Vague:** "Summarize this clinical note."

**Better:** "Summarize this clinical note in 3 sections: (1) Chief complaint and history, (2) Assessment and plan, (3) Critical flags and follow-up items. Each section should be 2–4 sentences."

### Give the model a role
Start your system prompt with a role. This anchors the model's behavior and reduces hallucination on domain-specific tasks.

"You are a board-certified emergency physician reviewing clinical notes for shift handoff."

vs.

"You are an AI assistant."

The first produces consistently better clinical output.

### Show, don't just tell (few-shot examples)
Including one or two examples of the input/output you want is often the single most effective technique.

```
System: You convert clinical abbreviations to plain English.

Example input: Pt c/o SOB x2d, +DOE, -orthopnea. PMH: HTN, DM2.
Example output: The patient reports difficulty breathing for 2 days that gets worse with exertion but does not prevent lying flat. They have a history of high blood pressure and type 2 diabetes.

Now convert:
[clinical text]
```

### Control output length
Specify how long you want the response. Models tend to be verbose by default.

"Respond in no more than 150 words."
"Be concise — 3 bullet points maximum."
"Provide a thorough, comprehensive answer — this will be used as a reference document."

### Chain of thought for complex reasoning
For complex clinical reasoning tasks, ask the model to think step by step before giving its answer.

"Before answering, think through the differential diagnosis systematically, then provide your final answer."

This significantly improves accuracy on reasoning tasks.

### Add safety rails
For healthcare applications, include explicit guardrails:

"Always include a disclaimer that this output is for informational purposes and should be reviewed by a qualified clinician before clinical use."

"If the user asks about a specific patient situation, always recommend they speak with their care provider rather than providing individualized medical advice."

## Iterating on your prompts

Getting a great prompt takes iteration. Here's the process:

1. Write your first prompt
2. Test it on 5–10 different inputs
3. Note where it fails or produces unexpected output
4. Add a rule or example to address that failure
5. Repeat until you're satisfied with 90%+ of outputs

Keep a document of your prompt iterations — it's your product's "source of truth" and will be valuable if you ever want to switch models or optimize further.

## Healthcare-specific prompting tips

**Always specify the intended audience.** "Explain this to a patient" vs. "Explain this to an emergency physician" produces very different output quality.

**Specify reading level for patient-facing content.** "Write at a 6th-grade reading level. Avoid medical jargon."

**Request citations or caveats on clinical claims.** "Cite the clinical guideline or evidence source for any factual claims. If uncertain, say so."

**Test with adversarial inputs.** What does your tool return if a user inputs something harmful, nonsensical, or outside its intended scope? Design for this — either in the prompt ("If the user asks about [out-of-scope topic], respond with [redirect]") or in your UI.

**Prefer "show your reasoning" for diagnostic tasks.** "Explain your reasoning before giving your final answer" produces outputs that clinicians can actually evaluate and trust.

## Common mistakes

**Too vague:** "Summarize this." → Give format, length, audience, and purpose.

**No role:** A generic "helpful assistant" will produce generic output. Give it a specific clinical role.

**No examples:** For structured output tasks, include at least one example of what "good" looks like.

**Assuming the model knows your context:** The model has no idea your product is for ICU nurses, or that your hospital uses EPIC, or that your users are non-English speakers. Tell it everything relevant.

**Optimizing too early:** Get it working first, then optimize. Don't spend three days perfecting your prompt before you have a product.
