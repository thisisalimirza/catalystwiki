A great Demo Day pitch tells a story. It doesn't just list features — it takes the audience on a journey from "here's a problem that matters" to "here's proof that we can fix it." This guide gives you a proven structure.

## The 7-slide structure (5–7 minutes)

### Slide 1: The hook (30 seconds)
Open with something concrete that makes the problem visceral. Not a definition — a moment.

**Examples:**
- "Every night, 400 ICU nurses in this country spend 45 minutes on shift handoffs that should take 10."
- "In 2024, 1 in 5 patients discharged from the hospital was readmitted within 30 days. Many of those readmissions were preventable — and they started with a patient who didn't understand their discharge instructions."
- "I'm a third-year resident. Last month, a patient I discharged ended up back in the ED 6 hours later because they couldn't understand the medication instructions we gave them. I've seen this happen dozens of times."

Don't start with "Hi, I'm [name], and our team is working on..." Start with the problem.

### Slide 2: The problem (45 seconds)
Make the problem specific and credible.

- Who has it? (Be specific — not "patients," but "post-op cardiac surgery patients being discharged from urban academic medical centers")
- How big is it? (Numbers: frequency, severity, cost)
- Why hasn't it been solved? (The constraint that makes this hard)

**Common mistake:** Being too abstract. "Healthcare communication is broken" is not a problem statement. "Physicians spend an average of 45 minutes per shift on documentation tasks that don't improve patient outcomes" is.

### Slide 3: The solution (60 seconds)
Now you earn the right to talk about what you built.

- One sentence describing what your product does
- Who uses it and how (the core workflow)
- Why your approach works (the insight behind it)

**Don't describe features — describe outcomes.** Not "our tool uses Claude to generate summaries" but "a nurse pastes in their patient notes, gets a structured handoff summary in 15 seconds, and finishes their shift handoff in under 5 minutes instead of 45."

### Slide 4: Live demo (2–3 minutes)
This is the most important part. Show the product actually working.

Narrate what you're doing as you go:
- "I'm now pasting in a real clinical note from one of our test cases..."
- "Within about 10 seconds, here's what the tool generates..."
- "You can see it's picked up on the critical findings the nurse flagged..."
- "If the clinician wants to edit or add context, they can do that here..."

**Demo tips:**
- Have your demo pre-loaded in a browser tab before you go on stage
- Use realistic (synthetic/de-identified) data, not placeholder text
- Show the happy path — the cleanest version of the core workflow
- Go slowly — the audience needs to track what's happening on screen

### Slide 5: Evidence (45 seconds)
What have you learned from real users?

- "We talked to 14 nurses across two hospitals..."
- "In user testing, all 5 users completed the core task in under 3 minutes vs. an average of 18 minutes before..."
- "Our sponsor, [company], gave us access to de-identified records to validate our approach..."
- "One of our test users said: '[quote]'"

User evidence transforms your pitch from an idea into something real.

### Slide 6: The path forward (30 seconds)
What would it take to make this real?

- What's the next version? (What would you build with more time?)
- What's the business model? (Who pays? Health system? Sponsor? Employer?)
- What's the regulatory path? (HIPAA-compliant storage, BAA with vendors, FDA classification)
- What's the go-to-market? (Pilot with your sponsor? Direct sales to health systems?)

You don't need all the answers. Show that you've thought about the questions.

### Slide 7: The ask (15 seconds)
End clearly. What do you want from this room?

- "We're looking for a pilot partner — if you know a health system that would benefit from this, we'd love an introduction."
- "We're exploring whether to continue this as a company. If you're an investor who focuses on clinical workflow, we'd love to talk."
- "We're looking for our next mentor — specifically someone who's done enterprise health system sales."

The ask focuses the post-presentation networking conversation.

## Slide design principles

**One idea per slide.** If you have more than one sentence on a slide that needs to be read by the audience, you have too much text.

**Big text, high contrast.** Your slides will be projected in a room with mixed lighting. 36pt minimum for body text. Dark text on light background.

**Data visualization over tables.** A chart showing 35% reduction in handoff time is more impactful than a table of numbers.

**Fewer slides, not more.** 7 slides is a maximum. 5 is better. Judges have seen 8-slide decks that felt like 20 and 20-slide decks that felt like 5. The number isn't the point — clarity is.

**Real screenshots, not mockups.** Show the actual product, not a Figma design, in the demo section. Judges know the difference.

## Handling Q&A

Q&A is where you either reinforce or undermine your pitch. Some guidance:

**Don't get defensive.** Hard questions are not attacks. They're judges doing their job — stress-testing your thinking. "That's a great point, let me address that..." is always the right opening.

**"I don't know" is a valid answer.** Better than speculation. "We haven't tested that yet, but here's how I'd think about it..."

**Know your numbers cold.** Sample size, frequency of the problem, usage data from testing, cost estimates. You don't need to have all of them — but the ones you have, you should know instantly.

**Common hard questions and how to handle them:**

| Question | Good answer approach |
|---|---|
| "Why wouldn't Epic/Cerner just build this?" | Explain the structural reason incumbents often don't solve edge cases. Point to your specific differentiation. |
| "How do you handle HIPAA?" | Describe your current approach (de-identified demo data) and your plan for a production deployment. |
| "What's your business model?" | It's okay to say "we're still figuring this out" — but say what the options are: per-seat SaaS, health system contract, sponsor acquisition. |
| "How accurate is the AI?" | Be specific. If you've tested it, give accuracy data. If you haven't, be honest and explain how you'd validate it. |
| "What about liability?" | Frame your tool as decision support requiring clinician review. No autonomous clinical decisions. |

## The one thing

If you remember nothing else from this guide: **practice out loud at least 5 times before Demo Day.** Not in your head — out loud, in front of people. The first time is painful. By the fifth time, you know your material and the Q&A won't scare you.

Your mentor will give you at least one feedback session on your pitch. Ask for it in week 9 if they don't initiate it.
