The live demo is the heart of your Demo Day presentation. This guide covers the technical and practical preparation needed to make it go smoothly.

## What "live demo" means

A live demo means you open your actual product in a browser and use it in real time in front of the judges. Not a video recording. Not a slide with screenshots. Not a Figma clickthrough.

This is a higher bar than you might be used to from class presentations — and it's one of the things that makes Demo Day exciting and credible.

## Demo structure (2–3 minutes)

Your demo should cover the **core user journey end-to-end**:

1. Show the user's starting point (the problem state)
2. Show how they interact with your product
3. Show the output or result
4. Show what the user does next

Example for a clinical note summarizer:
1. "Here's the note a nurse has at the end of their shift — 4 paragraphs of unstructured text." (Show it)
2. "She pastes it into our tool and clicks Summarize." (Do it live)
3. "10 seconds later, here's the structured SOAP summary." (Show it appearing)
4. "She reviews it, makes a quick edit, and copies it to her handoff sheet." (Do it)

Total time: ~90 seconds. The rest of your demo time can be used to show a second use case, highlight a key feature, or answer judge questions.

## Choosing what to demo

Demo the **happy path** — the core use case where everything works as intended, with realistic data.

**Do demo:**
- The most impressive, clearest example of your product doing what it's supposed to do
- Features that directly demonstrate your core value proposition
- A real (de-identified or synthetic) data example that makes the output look like the real world

**Don't demo:**
- Half-finished features
- Edge cases or error states
- The sign-up or onboarding flow (unless that IS the product)
- Anything you're not 100% confident works reliably

## Technical preparation

### 1. Use pre-loaded data
Don't type input during the demo. Pre-load your demo data in advance so you can paste it in with a single keystroke. The audience doesn't need to watch you type.

Keep a document open with:
- Your demo input text (the clinical note, the patient info, the question)
- Any secondary inputs for a second scenario

### 2. Test on the day-of machine
If you're using a shared conference laptop or projector, test your product on that machine before your slot. Log out of accounts you don't want visible. Close other browser tabs.

### 3. Use a guest browser profile
Create a guest browser profile or use incognito mode for your demo. This avoids autofill surprises, cached credentials from other accounts, and notifications popping up during the demo.

### 4. Test on the Demo Day WiFi
Conference WiFi is often slower than you expect. Test your app's performance on a mobile hotspot if you can — that's what you'll fall back to if the venue WiFi is slow. Make sure your API calls complete within an acceptable time on a slower connection.

### 5. Have a screen recording ready
Record a 2–3 minute screen recording of your demo (the happy path, narrated) using Loom or QuickTime. If your live demo fails, you can switch to the recording immediately.

Keep the recording tab open in your browser during your presentation. If anything goes wrong: "Let me show you a recording I made earlier..." Judges understand.

### 6. Check your API key and rate limits
The night before Demo Day, confirm your API key is active and your team has credits remaining. Log into the Anthropic console and verify.

If you're worried about hitting rate limits during the event (unlikely but possible if many teams are simultaneously demoing), contact your program facilitator — they can request a temporary limit increase.

## Rehearsal schedule

| When | What |
|---|---|
| Week 8 | First run-through with your team — just to get the words out |
| Week 9 | Mentor feedback session — practice full pitch + demo, get critique |
| Week 9 | Second practice with a different audience (another team, a friend, family member) |
| Day before Demo Day | Final run-through, check all tech, record your backup |
| Demo Day morning | One more run-through before doors open |

## Narrating the demo

During the live demo, narrate what you're doing and why it matters. Don't go silent while you click.

**Good narration:**
- "I'm pasting in a discharge note from a patient with complex comorbidities — this is the kind of note that typically takes a nurse 20 minutes to turn into a handoff document..."
- "You can see the AI has identified the critical findings and flagged the follow-up items in red — those are the things that can't fall through the cracks at shift change..."
- "If the nurse wants to add something the AI missed, she can edit inline here..."

**Avoid:**
- Going silent for more than 5 seconds
- Saying "um" repeatedly while waiting for the API to respond — narrate the wait: "While the model processes this — usually about 10 seconds — the nurse could be preparing the next patient's note..."
- Apologizing for the product ("sorry, this is a bit rough..."). Confidence.

## If something goes wrong

Stay calm. Judges have seen technical failures at every Demo Day they've ever attended. What they're evaluating is your composure and your knowledge — not whether your app has bugs.

Scenarios and responses:

**API times out:** "Our API provider occasionally has latency spikes — let me pull up the recording I made earlier that shows this exact scenario." Switch to recording.

**App doesn't load:** "We've been having some intermittent issues with our hosting — let me switch to a local version." Have a local fallback if possible.

**Unexpected error message:** "We have a known issue with this edge case — let me show you the flow with our test data instead." Have a backup input ready.

**Complete demo failure:** Stay calm, switch to your backup recording, and be honest: "We're having a technical issue. Let me show you a recording and walk you through what you're seeing." Then nail the Q&A.

The teams that handle failures gracefully often make a better impression than teams whose demos run flawlessly but whose founders don't really know their product.
